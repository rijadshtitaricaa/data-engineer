WHAT SQL MISTAKE I UNDERSTAND BETTER NOW:

- Incorrect JOIN conditions (wrong column matching) produces wrong results
- WHERE clauses encode business logic—status filtering is critical for revenue reporting

- Table aliases (o, c, p) are essential for clarity in multi-table queries
- GROUP BY and HAVING must appear in the correct order
- SUM(quantity * price) requires a JOIN because quantity and price are in different tables
- Without filtering for 'completed' status, revenue numbers are inflated and meaningless

Fixed Query 1 (Orders by City):
"This query counts orders by city. The city column is in the customers table, but orders are in the orders table. We JOIN them on customer_id to connect each order to its customer's city. GROUP BY city groups the order counts by each city. Results show Prishtina has 5 orders and Vushtrri has 4 orders—these are our strongest markets."

Fixed Query 7 (Completed Revenue):
"This calculates the total money we actually made. We multiply each order's quantity by the product's price. We JOIN orders to products using product_id. The WHERE status='completed' is critical—it excludes the $1,660 in pending and cancelled orders that aren't real revenue. Result is $1,639 in verified revenue."

Fixed Query 11 (Repeat Customers):
"We GROUP orders by customer_id and COUNT the orders per customer. HAVING COUNT > 1 filters to only customers with more than one order. Result shows 4 customers (Arta, Blend, Dren, Elira) each placed 2 orders. This is 40% retention rate, which is good for business."
