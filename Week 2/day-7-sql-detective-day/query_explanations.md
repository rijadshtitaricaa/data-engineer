## Fixed Queries Explanations

### Fixed Query 1: Orders by City
```sql
SELECT c.city, COUNT(*) AS order_count
FROM orders o
JOIN customers c
    ON o.customer_id = c.customer_id
GROUP BY c.city;
```

**What it does:** Counts how many orders were placed by customers from each city, grouped by location.

**Tables used:** `orders` and `customers`

**Why JOIN is needed:** The city information is stored in the `customers` table, but we need to count orders from the `orders` table. The JOIN connects these tables through `customer_id` so we can match each order to its customer's city. Without the JOIN, we would only have customer_id numbers with no way to know which city they're from.

**What the result means:** This reveals geographic distribution of order activity. In our data, Prishtina has 5 orders and Vushtrri has 4 orders, showing these are the strongest markets. Smaller cities like Peja and Prizren each have only 1 order, representing potential growth areas or underserved markets where the business could expand.

### Fixed Query 2: Revenue by Product Name
```sql
SELECT p.product_name, SUM(o.quantity * p.price) AS revenue
FROM orders o
JOIN products p  ON o.product_id = p.product_id
WHERE o.status = 'completed'
GROUP BY p.product_name;
```

**What it does:** Calculates the total revenue generated by each product, but only counting completed orders.

**Tables used:** `orders` and `products`

**Why JOIN is needed:** The product names and prices are in the `products` table, while quantities and order status are in the `orders` table. We need the JOIN to combine these pieces of information together. Additionally, we multiply quantity (from orders) by price (from products), which requires accessing both tables. Without the JOIN, we'd only have product IDs with no names or prices.

**What the result means:** Laptops generated $700 in revenue from a single completed order, making it the highest revenue product. Monitors generated $540 from multiple sales, showing strong volume. This helps identify which products are business drivers and which might need better marketing or pricing strategies.

### Fixed Query 3: Orders by Status
```sql
SELECT status, COUNT(*) AS order_count
FROM orders
GROUP BY status
ORDER BY order_count DESC;
```

**What it does:** Counts how many orders are in each status category (completed, pending, or cancelled) and sorts them from highest to lowest.

**Tables used:** `orders` only

**Why JOIN is not needed:** All the information we need—the status column and the order IDs to count—are already in the `orders` table. We don't need to reference any other table, so a JOIN would be unnecessary and would only slow down the query.

**What the result means:** We have 10 completed orders (71%), 2 pending orders (14%), and 2 cancelled orders (14%). This shows that most orders are successfully completed, but 28% of orders are either stuck in pending or have been cancelled. This suggests the business has a decent completion rate but should investigate why some orders are stuck pending or getting cancelled.

### Fixed Query 4: Total Amount per Order
```sql
SELECT o.order_id, o.quantity, p.price,
 o.quantity * p.price AS total_amount
FROM orders o JOIN products p on o.product_id = p.product_id;
```

**What it does:** Shows each order's ID, the quantity ordered, the unit price, and calculates the total monetary value of each individual order.

**Tables used:** `orders` and `products`

**Why JOIN is needed:** The quantity is in the `orders` table, but the price is in the `products` table. To calculate the total amount (quantity × price), we must JOIN these tables by matching `product_id`. Without the JOIN, we'd have the quantity but no way to get the price needed for the calculation.

**What the result means:** This reveals the monetary value of each transaction. Order 1 is worth $700 (1 Laptop), while Order 2 is worth $30 (2 Mice). This helps identify high-value orders that might need special attention or VIP customer treatment, and helps with inventory planning based on order sizes.

### Fixed Query 5: Quantity by Category
```sql
SELECT p.category, SUM(quantity) AS total_quantity
FROM orders o JOIN products p ON p.product_id = o.product_id
WHERE o.status = 'completed'
GROUP BY category;
```

**What it does:** Totals the number of units sold in each product category, counting only completed orders.

**Tables used:** `orders` and `products`

**Why JOIN is needed:** Categories are stored in the `products` table while quantities are in the `orders` table. The JOIN connects them by `product_id` so we can see which categories had units sold. We also add a WHERE filter to only count completed orders, because pending and cancelled orders don't represent actual inventory that left the warehouse.

**What the result means:** Electronics had 2 units sold, Accessories had 13 units sold, and Office category had 1 unit sold. This shows that while Electronics generates the most revenue, Accessories actually move more units. This suggests different pricing strategies: high-price, low-volume for electronics versus low-price, high-volume for accessories.

### Fixed Query 6: Total Revenue
```sql
SELECT SUM(quantity * price) AS total_revenue
FROM orders
JOIN products ON orders.product_id = products.product_id;
```

**What it does:** Calculates the total dollar value of all orders combined by multiplying quantity and price for each order, then summing them up.

**Tables used:** `orders` and `products`

**Why JOIN is needed:** Quantities come from the `orders` table but prices come from the `products` table. To multiply them together for each order, we must JOIN these tables using `product_id`. Without the JOIN, we'd have no way to match quantities with their corresponding prices.

**What the result means:** The total of all orders (including pending and cancelled) is worth $3,299. However, only $1,639 of this is actual completed revenue—the other $1,660 is either pending completion or was cancelled. This metric helps executives understand the total size of the business opportunity, even though only part of it has been realized.

### Fixed Query 7: Customers with Multiple Orders
```sql
SELECT customer_id, COUNT(*) AS order_count 
FROM orders 
GROUP BY customer_id
HAVING COUNT(*) > 1;
```

**What it does:** Finds which customers have placed more than one order and shows how many orders each repeat customer made.

**Tables used:** `orders` only

**Why JOIN is not needed:** We only need customer IDs and order counts, both from the `orders` table. The HAVING clause filters to only customers with more than 1 order. No other table is needed for this analysis.

**What the result means:** 4 customers (40% of active customers) have placed more than one order. Customer 1, 2, 3, and 4 each have 2 orders. This indicates decent customer loyalty and repeat purchase behavior. These repeat customers are valuable—they could be targets for loyalty programs or premium service offerings to increase their lifetime value.

### Fixed Query 8: Orders with Customer Names
```sql
SELECT orders.order_id, customers.customer_name
FROM orders
JOIN customers on orders.customer_id = customers.customer_id;
```

**What it does:** Lists each order ID alongside the name of the customer who placed that order.

**Tables used:** `orders` and `customers`

**Why JOIN is needed:** Order IDs are in the `orders` table but customer names are in the `customers` table. The JOIN connects them through `customer_id`. Without this JOIN, we'd only see order IDs paired with numeric customer IDs, which are not human-readable and make it hard to understand who placed which order.

**What the result means:** This creates a customer-facing view of orders that's useful for order tracking, customer service, and shipping labels. For example, we can see that Order 1 belongs to Arta and Order 2 belongs to Blend. This mapping is essential for operations teams to actually fulfill orders to the correct customers.

## Validation Queries Explanations

### Validation Query V1: Total Orders Count
```sql
SELECT COUNT(*) AS total_orders FROM orders;
```

**What it does:** Counts the total number of order records in the entire orders table, regardless of status.

**Tables used:** `orders` only

**Why JOIN is not needed:** We're just counting rows in one table. No other information is needed from other tables.

**What the result means:** There are 14 total orders in the system. This is the baseline metric for business activity. We use this to calculate percentages: for example, 10 completed orders out of 14 total = 71% completion rate. It also establishes the scale of the business and helps with capacity planning.

---

### Validation Query V7: Completed Revenue
```sql
SELECT SUM(o.quantity * p.price) AS completed_revenue FROM orders o
JOIN products p ON o.product_id = p.product_id
WHERE o.status = 'completed';
```

**What it does:** Calculates the dollar value of all orders that have been completed, excluding pending and cancelled orders.

**Tables used:** `orders` and `products`

**Why JOIN is needed:** We need to multiply each order's quantity (from orders table) by the product's price (from products table). The JOIN connects these using `product_id`. The WHERE clause is critical here—it filters to only 'completed' status so we don't count revenue from orders that didn't actually go through.

**What the result means:** The business has generated $1,639 in verified revenue. This is the "real" number for financial reporting because only completed orders represent actual money received. The remaining $1,660 in order value is either pending (customer might cancel) or already cancelled (lost revenue). This is the number used in investor reports and financial statements.

### Validation Query V9: Revenue by Category
```sql
SELECT p.category, SUM(o.quantity * p.price) AS revenue
FROM orders o
JOIN products p ON o.product_id = p.product_id
WHERE o.status = 'completed'
GROUP BY p.category;
```

**What it does:** Groups completed orders by product category and calculates total revenue for each category.

**Tables used:** `orders` and `products`

**Why JOIN is needed:** Categories exist in the `products` table while order quantities are in the `orders` table. The JOIN matches each order to its product category. Then we calculate revenue (quantity × price) and GROUP BY to sum it up per category.

**What the result means:** Electronics generated $1,240 (75.7%), Accessories generated $279 (17%), and Office generated $120 (7.3%). This shows that Electronics is the clear revenue winner. This information guides inventory decisions, marketing budgets, and supplier relationships. If Electronics is your profit center, you want to ensure supply chain stability for these products.

### Validation Query V10: Orders by City
```sql
SELECT c.city, COUNT(*) AS order_count
FROM orders o
JOIN customers c ON o.customer_id = c.customer_id
GROUP BY c.city;
```

**What it does:** Groups all orders by the city where the customer is located and counts how many orders come from each city.

**Tables used:** `orders` and `customers`

**Why JOIN is needed:** Cities are stored in the `customers` table (because each customer has a city), but orders are in the `orders` table. To connect orders to cities, we must JOIN through `customer_id`. Without this JOIN, we'd have no way to know the city for each order.

**What the result means:** Prishtina accounts for 5 orders (36% of total), Vushtrri has 4 orders (29%), and other cities have smaller counts. This geographic concentration tells us that Prishtina and Vushtrri are the core markets where the business should maintain strong inventory and service levels. The cities with only 1 order each might be one-time or sporadic customers.

### Validation Query V13: Non-Revenue Orders
```sql
SELECT * FROM orders
WHERE status IN ('pending', 'cancelled');
```

**What it does:** Retrieves all order details for orders that are either still pending (not yet completed) or have been cancelled.

**Tables used:** `orders` only

**Why JOIN is not needed:** We're just selecting all columns from the orders table and filtering by status. We don't need customer names, product details, or any other information to identify which orders are at risk.

**What the result means:** There are 4 orders (28% of total) that don't count as revenue: 2 cancelled orders and 2 pending orders. These represent $1,660 in lost or uncertain revenue. The cancelled orders are definite losses (should investigate why), while pending orders are at-risk revenue that might still convert if customers follow through. This metric highlights an important business problem: nearly 1 in 3 orders aren't completing successfully.
